# EVAL-074 — Methodology audit (cross-judge rescore)

**Status:** RETRACTION — original EVAL-074 conclusion (PR #549) is **not supported** by the cross-judge audit. The "DeepSeek lessons-block regression on gotcha tasks" finding is a Llama-3.3-70B judge artifact at this evidence level.
**Date:** 2026-04-26
**Supersedes (in part):** [`docs/eval/EVAL-074-deepseek-root-cause.md`](EVAL-074-deepseek-root-cause.md) (PR #549, 2026-04-26).
**Source data:** unchanged — same n=200 JSONL at `docs/archive/eval-runs/eval-071-2026-04-22/eval-071-deepseek-v3-1-ab-n100-1776740166.jsonl`.

## What changed

The original EVAL-074 result doc shipped a "-30pp gotcha-only correctness regression" claim with McNemar p=0.0007, based on the single Llama-3.3-70B judge that came with the EVAL-071 sweep. After shipping, a cross-judge audit was performed in response to a methodology challenge.

Two judges were attempted:
- **claude-sonnet-4-5** (Anthropic, cross-family) — 200 rows attempted, 161 successfully scored (80%; 39 nulls likely safety filter or transient API).
- **Qwen-2.5-72B-Instruct-Turbo** (Alibaba, cross-family) — blocked by Together credit limit (`Credit limit exceeded`). 0/200 scored. Filed as follow-up below.

The Sonnet rescore alone is decisive enough to retract.

## Headline numbers

### A/B delta per judge

| Judge | Slice | Cell A | Cell B | ΔBA | Paired -:+ | McNemar p |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Llama-3.3-70B** (original) | clean | 38% | 40% | +2pp | 4:5 | 1.000 |
| **Llama-3.3-70B** (original) | **gotcha** | 68% | 38% | **−30pp** | **17:2** | **0.0007** |
| **Llama-3.3-70B** (original) | overall | 53% | 39% | −14pp | 21:7 | 0.0125 |
| **Sonnet-4.5** (rescore) | clean | 29% | 28% | −0.3pp | 2:1 | 1.000 |
| **Sonnet-4.5** (rescore) | **gotcha** | 29% | 29% | **−0.4pp** | **2:2** | **1.000** |
| **Sonnet-4.5** (rescore) | overall | 29% | 28% | −0.4pp | 4:3 | 1.000 |

**The gotcha-only effect Llama saw at p=0.0007 is invisible to Sonnet.** Sonnet sees DeepSeek as broadly mediocre (29% on both cells, both categories) with no lessons-block effect in any direction.

### Judge agreement structure

Among 161 rows both judges scored:

- **Overall agreement: 71%** (Cohen's κ = **0.40** — "fair" by Landis & Koch, well below the κ ≥ 0.6 "substantial" threshold the project uses for cross-judge validation in EVAL-073).
- **Llama-lenient pattern: 38 rows Llama-pass / Sonnet-fail** vs. **9 rows Sonnet-lenient**. Llama lets ~4× more rows through than Sonnet on this fixture.
- **Disagreement is concentrated where the original signal lives.**

| Slice | Agreement |
|---|---|
| Cell A only | 61% (49/80) |
| Cell B only | 80% (65/81) |
| **Clean tasks** | **86%** (76/88) |
| **Gotcha tasks** | **52%** (38/73) |

Clean tasks: judges broadly agree. Gotcha tasks: judges agree barely above chance — and gotcha is exactly where the original "regression" was concentrated. The original finding's "p=0.0007 effect on gotcha" is co-located with the precise subgroup where the underlying judge agreement is worst.

## What this means for the original conclusion

The original EVAL-074 doc framed the regression as a **model behavior** — DeepSeek over-complies on gotcha tasks under the lessons block, skipping safety/clarification rubric requirements. Sonnet's data is **incompatible** with that framing:

- If DeepSeek truly skipped clarification on `gotcha-08-budget-exhaustion`, Sonnet should also score cell B as a fail.
- Sonnet's rubric application is, on the gotcha subgroup, ~50% different from Llama's. Either Sonnet is systematically wrong (no a-priori reason to prefer it), or Llama is, or both judges are seeing different rubric criteria.

The most parsimonious read: **Llama-3.3-70B's interpretation of the gotcha rubric is form-sensitive** — short cell-A "I can't run that" responses score as "asks for clarification", longer cell-B "Here's how / here's the warning" responses score as "skipped the clarification step". Sonnet, applying the same written rubric, doesn't draw the line in the same place.

This does not prove DeepSeek has no lessons-block effect. It proves **the published evidence does not establish one**. The signal observable at this evidence level is not a model effect; it is a judge effect.

## What's still defensible from the original analysis

Independent of the correctness regression, the descriptive observations remain:

1. **DeepSeek cell B responses are 2× longer** than cell A (731 → 1417 chars). This is a measurable behavioral change from the lessons block, judge-independent.
2. **Two clean-task outliers produced corrupted off-topic content** (`clean-04`, `clean-06`). Real artifact, judge-independent. May or may not be lessons-block-induced; n=2.
3. **A/A correctness noise floor on this fixture is essentially zero** at n=100 (gotcha A/A: 1↔1 paired flips, ΔBA = 0pp, McNemar p=1.0). This was new analysis added during the audit and is robust — A/A is a within-judge measurement and doesn't depend on cross-judge agreement.

The **mechanism claim** (over-compliance on gotcha) is retracted. The **descriptive claim** (lessons block roughly doubles DeepSeek response length) stands.

## Implications beyond EVAL-074

This is a load-bearing finding for the project's cross-architecture eval program:

1. **EVAL-071's headline conclusion** ("F2 does not extend to non-Anthropic frontier; lessons hurt DeepSeek correctness by -14pp") rests on the same single Llama judge. The hallucination finding ("0% in both cells") is judge-independent. The correctness finding is not.
2. **EVAL-075's "refusal_with_instruction" axis** was scored on the same JSONL — its A=15% B=8% inversion may also reflect Llama-form-sensitivity rather than DeepSeek behavior.
3. **EVAL-073's claim of 100% cross-judge agreement** under strict binary rubric (Sonnet vs. Llama on n=90) does not generalize — at minimum, this audit shows agreement is **fixture- and architecture-dependent**, not a property of the rubric alone.
4. **RESEARCH-021's prereg** specifies a 3-judge cross-family panel for exactly this reason. This audit operationalizes that prereg's premise: single-judge sweeps on cross-family agents are unsafe to draw conclusions from.

## What should have happened

The original EVAL-074 doc attempted MVP discipline (no new sweep, root-cause from existing data). That was the right intent. The wrong choice was treating "single-judge result" as sufficient to publish a mechanism claim. The audit should have been part of the original deliverable, not a separate retraction.

For future analysis-only root-causes on existing data, the standing rule should be: **any claim that depends on judge labels must include a cross-judge audit on the same JSONL before it is stamped as a result**. Cross-judge rescoring is cheap (this audit cost ~$1.50 in Anthropic API spend) and is the difference between "a model effect" and "a Llama effect."

## Limitations of the audit itself

- Sonnet null rate of 19.5% (39/200) — likely safety filter on some agent outputs. Treating nulls as missing rather than disagreements is the conservative choice; even so, the both-scored 71% agreement / κ=0.40 is well below the project's 80% / 0.6 thresholds.
- Single Anthropic judge. The audit shows two judges disagree, not which one is "correct." A third (Qwen) was attempted and is filed as follow-up.
- Same fixture, same agent run. The audit applies to *this* JSONL; it does not establish a general finding about Llama-as-judge across the project's eval portfolio. It does establish that Llama-as-sole-judge is unsafe for cross-architecture A/B claims on this fixture.
